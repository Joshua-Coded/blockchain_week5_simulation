Scenario

A fictional NGO is delivering food and medical aid to displaced persons in a conflict region. The problem: funds keep disappearing, beneficiary identities cannot be verified, and no one agrees on who received what. Your class will try to solve this — first with a centralized system, then with blockchain, then with a smart contract that enforces rules automatically.

How to Facilitate

  • Open the Roles page and assign each student or group a role before anything else.
  • Go to Round 1. Submit a few transactions. Then let the Bad Actor press "Tamper with Record." Discuss what the Auditor can and cannot see.
  • Move to Round 2. Now every transaction requires a node vote. Try the same tamper attack — the chain detects it.
  • Move to Round 3. The smart contract adds automated rules. Try an unverified ID or a suspicious purpose.
  • End on the Debrief page. Questions connect the simulation back to BanQu, AidCoin, and your slide 10 discussion prompts.

Round 1 — Centralized

One coordinator runs the ledger. No checks. The Bad Actor alters records without detection. The Auditor is almost always too late.

Round 2 — Blockchain

Transactions broadcast to all nodes. 2 of 3 must approve. Tampering breaks the chain hash and is detected automatically.

Round 3 — Smart Contract

Automated rules enforce verified IDs and legitimate purposes before the transaction even reaches the nodes.

Debrief

Six discussion questions connecting simulation outcomes to BanQu, AidCoin, and blockchain applications across Africa.

Step 1 — Before You Begin

Assign Roles

Assign each student or group a role before starting Round 1. Students playing Node roles should ideally sit together so they can vote independently.

Role Responsibility in the Simulation Suggested Size
Donor submits Decides how much money to send and to which beneficiary. Watches to see if funds actually arrive correctly. 1 – 2
NGO Coordinator submits Fills in transaction details — purpose, beneficiary ID, amount — and submits to the ledger on behalf of the donor. 1 – 2
Node Alpha votes Receives every transaction and votes to approve or reject it. Represents one computer on the network. 1 – 3
Node Beta votes Second independent node. Votes separately. Two of three nodes must agree before a block is recorded. 1 – 3
Node Gamma votes Third node. Try making this node hostile in Round 2 — see whether two honest nodes can still reach consensus. 1 – 3
Beneficiary receives Holds a digital ID card — verified or unverified. Their ID determines whether Round 3 will accept a transaction made in their name. 2 – 4
Bad Actor attacks Tries to submit fraudulent transactions or tamper with existing records. In Round 1 this usually works. In Round 2 and 3 it doesn't. 1 – 2
Auditor reviews Reviews the ledger after each round. In Round 1: very hard to catch tampering. In Round 2: trivial — the ledger is immutable. 1 – 2

Beneficiary ID Cards

Tell students playing Beneficiaries which ID they hold. This becomes important in Round 3 when the smart contract checks for verification.

REF-001

Verified identity. All transactions to this ID will pass the smart contract check in Round 3.

REF-002

Verified identity. Also passes all checks. Use for a second legitimate beneficiary.

REF-999

Unverified. The smart contract will auto-reject any transaction to this ID in Round 3.

REF-000

Fake ID created by the Bad Actor. Always rejected in Round 3 regardless of purpose.

Round 1 — Centralized

Q 01
What happened when the Bad Actor tampered with the ledger? Who noticed — and at what point? What would have happened if there was no Auditor at all?
Q 02
In real humanitarian aid delivery, who plays the role of the single coordinator? Name an organization or system. What are the risks of giving any single actor that level of unchecked control?

Round 2 — Blockchain

Q 03
Why could the Bad Actor not tamper successfully in Round 2? What specifically broke — and how did the system detect it without a human auditor involved?
Q 04
We used 3 nodes and required 2 to agree. What happens if one node is dishonest? What if two nodes collude? At what point does the consensus model fail?

Round 3 — Smart Contract

Q 05
The smart contract auto-rejected transactions with unverified IDs. But who wrote those rules? Who has the authority to update them — and what happens if the rules themselves are wrong or biased against certain populations?
Q 06
Think about BanQu from the case study. How would you adapt this three-round simulation to model refugee identity management in East Africa? Which of the three rounds best represents what BanQu actually does — and what is still missing?
